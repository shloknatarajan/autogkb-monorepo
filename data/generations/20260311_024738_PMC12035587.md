## Background
This article is a case report describing severe toxicity after starting mercaptopurine, a thiopurine used in maintenance therapy for acute promyelocytic leukemia. A 41-year-old South Asian woman developed major adverse effects 7 weeks after treatment initiation, including profound pancytopenia, elevated liver enzymes, mucositis, and marked alopecia. To investigate drug-related susceptibility, the patient underwent pharmacogenetic testing for thiopurine-related variants.

## Key Findings
Genetic testing showed **wild-type TPMT** and **homozygous loss-of-function NUDT15 c.415C>T (*3/*3)**, a genotype associated with **complete loss of NUDT15 function**. In this patient, the **NUDT15*3/*3** genotype was linked to **severe mercaptopurine intolerance**, with clinically significant **myelosuppression** (Hb 60 g/L, WBC 0.9 × 10^9/L, platelets 4 × 10^9/L, ANC 0), **hepatotoxicity** (ALT 82 U/L), and **alopecia** after standard therapy. Mercaptopurine was discontinued, and because of the high risk of recurrent toxicity, it was omitted from future treatment.

No new statistical analysis was performed in this paper because it reports a single patient. However, within the article’s reported literature context, **individuals with homozygous NUDT15*3 reportedly tolerate only about 8% of the standard mercaptopurine dose before significant toxicity develops**. The case also reinforces that **TPMT-negative patients can still experience life-threatening thiopurine toxicity if they carry NUDT15 risk variants**, highlighting the clinical relevance of **NUDT15 genotyping**, especially in patients of Asian ancestry.
