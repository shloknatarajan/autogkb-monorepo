## Summary

## Background
This case-control pharmacogenomic study examined genetic risk factors for severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCAR) caused by allopurinol in Han Chinese patients in Taiwan. The investigators studied 51 patients with allopurinol-induced SCAR (including HSS, SJS, and TEN), 135 allopurinol-tolerant patients, and 93 population controls. They first screened 823 SNPs in immune-, MHC-, and drug metabolism-related genes, then performed high-resolution HLA typing.

## Key Findings
- **Strong HLA association with allopurinol-induced SCAR:** The clearest pharmacogenomic signal was in the HLA region, especially HLA-B*58:01.
  - **HLA-B*58:01:** Present in 51/51 SCAR cases (100%), compared with 20/135 allopurinol-tolerant controls (15%) and 19/93 population controls (20%). This was the strongest association in the study:
    - vs tolerant controls: OR 580.3, 95% CI 34.4–9780.9, corrected P = 4.7 × 10^-24
    - vs population controls: OR 393.5, 95% CI 23.2–6665.26, corrected P = 8.1 × 10^-18
  - **HLA-C*03:02:** Also strongly enriched in SCAR cases:
    - 48/51 cases (94%) vs 19/135 tolerant controls (14%): OR 97.7, corrected P = 1.4 × 10^-19
    - 48/51 cases (94%) vs 19/93 population controls (20%): OR 62.3, corrected P = 2.5 × 10^-13
  - **HLA-A*33:03:** Increased in SCAR cases:
    - 34/51 cases (67%) vs 24/135 tolerant controls (18%): OR 9.3, corrected P = 2.2 × 10^-4
    - 34/51 cases (67%) vs 20/93 population controls (22%): OR 7.3, corrected P = 4.7 × 10^-2
  - **HLA-DRB1*03:01:** Increased in SCAR cases:
    - 33/51 cases (65%) vs 17/135 tolerant controls (13%): OR 12.7, corrected P = 2.8 × 10^-6
    - 33/51 cases (65%) vs 14/93 population controls (15%): OR 10.3, corrected P = 8.5 × 10^-4

- **Extended HLA haplotype signal:** Several associated HLA alleles appeared to travel together, consistent with linkage disequilibrium around HLA-B*58:01.
  - **HLA-A*33:03–C*03:02–B*58:01–DRB1*03:01 haplotype:** Observed in 21/51 SCAR cases (41%), compared with 9/135 tolerant controls (7%) and 9/93 population controls (10%).
  - **B*58:01/C*03:02 combination:** Found in 48/51 cases (94%) vs 19/135 tolerant controls (14%) and 19/93 population controls (20%):
    - OR 97.7, corrected P = 1.4 × 10^-19 vs tolerant controls
    - OR 62.3, corrected P = 2.6 × 10^-13 vs population controls

- **Genome-wide candidate SNP screening highlighted the MHC region:** In the initial 823-SNP screen, the strongest SNP associations were in the MHC rather than in allopurinol metabolism genes.
  - **BAT3 rs3117583, MSH5 rs1150793, MICB rs2855804:** These MHC-region SNPs had the most significant screening signals, each with P < 10^-7 in the initial analysis.
  - **CFLAR rs2268791 and rs1594:** These non-MHC SNPs showed potential association in the screening stage and were supported in follow-up genotyping, although detailed effect sizes were not provided in the Results text.
  - **KIR2DL1 rs2304224:** Showed a possible signal in the initial screen but was not confirmed in the replication stage.
  - **Allopurinol metabolism genes:** No significant associations were reported for SNPs in genes involved in allopurinol metabolism, including xanthine oxidase, aldehyde oxidase, purine nucleoside phosphorylase, and hypoxanthine phosphoribosyl transferase.

- **Clinical risk context alongside genetic findings:** A non-genetic risk factor was also identified in the case-control comparison.
  - **Chronic renal insufficiency:** More common in SCAR cases than tolerant controls and associated with higher risk of allopurinol-SCAR (OR 4.7, 95% CI 2.3–9.3). This is a clinical covariate rather than a pharmacogenomic marker, but it provides context for risk assessment.
