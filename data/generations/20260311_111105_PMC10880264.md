# Pharmacogenetic Factors Influence Escitalopram Pharmacokinetics and Adverse Events in Youth with a Family History of Bipolar Disorder: A Preliminary Study

## Summary

## Background
This preliminary pharmacogenomic study evaluated escitalopram-treated adolescents (ages 12–17) with depression and/or anxiety who also had a first-degree relative with bipolar I disorder. Participants underwent pharmacogenetic testing for **CYP2C19**, **CYP2D6**, **HTR2A** (rs6311, −1438G>A), and **SLC6A4**, and a subset also had serum escitalopram levels measured. The study examined whether these genetic factors were linked to escitalopram pharmacokinetics or treatment-emergent adverse effects.

## Key Findings
- **Escitalopram pharmacokinetics:** Genetic effects were seen for drug exposure mainly with **CYP2C19**, not **CYP2D6**.
  - **CYP2C19 phenotype:** Slower metabolizers had significantly higher dose-normalized escitalopram exposure, including:
    - higher **AUC0–24** (*p* = 0.025)
    - higher **trough concentration (Ctrough)** (*p* = 0.013)
    - longer **elimination half-life (t1/2)** (*p* < 0.001)
    - A trend toward higher **Cmax** was also seen, but this was not statistically significant (*p* = 0.057).
  - **CYP2D6 phenotype:** No significant association was found with escitalopram pharmacokinetic measures:
    - **AUC0–24** (*p* = 0.52)
    - **Cmax** (*p* = 0.48)
    - **Ctrough** (*p* = 0.56)
    - **t1/2** (*p* = 0.50)

- **Adverse event risk during escitalopram treatment:** Some adverse-effect signals were linked to **CYP2D6** and **HTR2A**, but not to **CYP2C19** or **SLC6A4**.
  - **CYP2D6 phenotype:** Slower metabolizers had higher activation-related adverse effect scores on the TEASAP:
    - increased **akathisia/hyperkinesis/somatic anxiety** scores (*p* = 0.015)
    - when grouped as slower-than-normal vs normal/faster metabolizers, slower metabolizers also had higher **disinhibition/impulsivity** scores (mean 3.0 vs 1.1, *p* = 0.02)
    - no significant association was seen with hyperarousal events overall (*p* = 0.55) or maximum PAERS score (*p* = 0.51)
  - **HTR2A rs6311 (−1438G>A):** Participants with **A/A or A/G** genotypes had greater increases in TEASAP **“self-injury, suicidality, and harm to others”** scores than those with **G/G** (*p* = 0.017).
    - No significant association was seen with other TEASAP subscales (*p* = 0.15–0.45), maximum PAERS score (*p* = 0.39), or hyperarousal events (*p* = 0.53).
  - **CYP2C19 phenotype:** Despite clear effects on pharmacokinetics, **CYP2C19** was not significantly associated with adverse outcomes, including:
    - hyperarousal events (*p* = 0.60)
    - maximum PAERS score (*p* = 0.27)
    - TEASAP subscales (*p* = 0.39–0.92), although self-injury/suicidality/harm-to-others scores showed a nonsignificant trend (*p* = 0.09)
  - **SLC6A4 genotype:** No significant association was found between **S/S** versus **L/L or L/S** and adverse outcomes:
    - TEASAP subscales (*p* = 0.40–0.81)
    - maximum PAERS score (*p* = 0.72)
    - hyperarousal events (*p* = 0.72)

- **Drug exposure versus adverse events:** Measured escitalopram exposure itself was not clearly linked to adverse effects.
  - **Escitalopram Cmax and AUC0–24:** Neither was significantly associated with hyperarousal or TEASAP subscale changes.
    - hyperarousal: **Cmax** (*p* = 0.92), **AUC0–24** (*p* = 0.74)
    - TEASAP subscales: **Cmax** (*p* = 0.16–0.77), **AUC0–24** (*p* = 0.34–1.00)

Overall, the study supports **CYP2C19** as an important determinant of escitalopram exposure in youth, while **slower CYP2D6 metabolism** and **HTR2A rs6311 A-allele carriage** were the main genetic signals associated with specific adverse-effect patterns during treatment.
