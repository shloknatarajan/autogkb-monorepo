## Summary

## Background
This study examined whether genetic variation in Europeans influences the risk of hypersensitivity reactions to carbamazepine. The investigators performed genome-wide association studies in patients of European ancestry with carbamazepine-induced hypersensitivity syndrome, maculopapular exanthema, or Stevens–Johnson syndrome/toxic epidermal necrolysis (SJS/TEN), using population controls and carbamazepine-tolerant clinical controls. HLA alleles were assessed by SNP-based imputation and confirmed with high-resolution HLA typing.

## Key Findings
The main pharmacogenomic finding was a strong association between **HLA-A*31:01** and **carbamazepine-induced hypersensitivity** in Europeans. In the genome-wide analysis of hypersensitivity syndrome, the signal mapped to the HLA-A region, and **HLA-A*31:01** was significantly associated with risk (**P = 3.5 × 10^-8**); the allele was present in **40.0% of cases vs 4.9% of controls**. In an independent genome-wide analysis of **maculopapular exanthema**, **HLA-A*31:01** was again the strongest signal (**P = 1.11 × 10^-6**), seen in **27% of cases vs 4% of controls**.

Follow-up genotyping using carbamazepine-tolerant clinical controls confirmed that **HLA-A*31:01** increased risk across multiple hypersensitivity phenotypes: **hypersensitivity syndrome** (**OR 12.41**, 95% CI 1.27–121.03), **maculopapular exanthema** (**OR 8.33**, 95% CI 3.59–19.36; **P = 8.0 × 10^-7**), and **SJS/TEN** (**OR 25.93**, 95% CI 4.93–116.18; **P = 8.0 × 10^-5**). In pooled analysis of all hypersensitivity cases, **HLA-A*31:01** was associated with overall carbamazepine hypersensitivity (**OR 9.12**, 95% CI 4.03–20.65; **P = 1.0 × 10^-7**). The study also showed that **rs1061235** is a reliable proxy marker for **HLA-A*31:01** in Europeans, with **complete linkage disequilibrium (r² = 1)** and 100% concordance with direct HLA typing.
