## Summary

## Background
This study developed and tested a warfarin dose-refinement algorithm for Caribbean Hispanic patients from Puerto Rico. The authors analyzed 255 patients in a derivation cohort and externally validated the model in 55 additional Puerto Rican patients. Genotyping focused on key warfarin-related genes, mainly **CYP2C9, VKORC1, CYP4F2,** and **NQO1**, and the model also included an individual ancestry/admixture measure plus early clinical response data.

## Key Findings
The strongest pharmacogenomic signals were in **CYP2C9** and **VKORC1**, both associated with **lower warfarin dose requirements**. Compared with wild-type, **CYP2C9*2** was associated with a **7% lower dose** (**p=0.014**) and **CYP2C9*3** with a **16% lower dose** (**p=0.015**). **CYP2C9*8** also showed a trend toward lower dose (about **31% lower**, **p=0.098**), but this did not reach statistical significance. For **VKORC1 -1639G>A (rs9923231)**, both variant genotypes were associated with lower dose: **GA** was linked to a reduction (**p=0.007**) and **AA** to a larger reduction (**p<0.0001**); overall, each **A allele** was associated with about a **13% lower warfarin dose**.

Two additional variants were associated with **higher warfarin dose requirements**. **CYP4F2*3 (rs2108622)** increased dose by about **17% per variant allele** (**p=0.014**). **NQO1*2 (rs1800566)** increased dose by about **10% per variant allele**, but this was **not statistically significant** in the final model (**p=0.125**). Rare variants such as **CYP2C9*4, *5, *6, *11** and **VKORC1 p.V45A (rs104894540)** were identified in a few patients, but the paper did not show robust dose-association testing for these rare genotypes.

Overall, the admixture-adjusted pharmacogenomic model explained **70% of warfarin dose variability** in the derivation cohort (**R²=0.70, MAE 0.72 mg/day**) versus **60%** for a clinical-only model. In external validation, the pharmacogenomic model explained **about 58–59%** of dose variability (**MAE 0.89 mg/day**). This supports that, in this population, adding **CYP2C9, VKORC1, CYP4F2**, and ancestry information improved prediction of warfarin maintenance dose, especially for patients needing unusually low or high doses.
