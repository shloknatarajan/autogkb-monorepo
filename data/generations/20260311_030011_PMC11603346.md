## Summary

## Background
This systematic review and meta-analysis examined whether **CYP2B6** genetic variation is associated with **NNRTI-related drug-induced liver injury (DILI)** in people living with HIV treated with **efavirenz (EFV)** or **nevirapine (NVP)**. From 50 papers reviewed overall, **CYP2B6** was one of the most frequently reported genes linked to antiretroviral DILI. For the meta-analysis, the authors included **3 EFV studies** (106 DILI cases among 795 patients; all in African populations) and **2 NVP studies** (38 DILI cases and 782 controls; African and Caucasian populations). Pooled odds ratios were calculated using a fixed-effects Mantel-Haenszel model, and heterogeneity was low (**I² = 0%** in all pooled analyses).

## Key Findings
The main pharmacogenomic signal involved the **CYP2B6*6** reduced-function genotype group, assessed against **CYP2B6*1/*1** (normal metabolizer). For **efavirenz-induced liver injury**, carriers of **CYP2B6*1/*6** had a significantly higher risk (**OR 1.83, 95% CI 1.15–2.90, p=0.01**), and **CYP2B6*6/*6** homozygotes had an even higher risk (**OR 2.48, 95% CI 1.28–4.79, p=0.007**). When **CYP2B6*1/*6 and *6/*6** were combined, the association remained significant (**OR 1.94, 95% CI 1.24–3.01, p=0.003**).

For **nevirapine-induced liver injury**, the pattern was different. **CYP2B6*1/*6** was associated with a **lower** risk compared with **CYP2B6*1/*1** (**OR 0.44, 95% CI 0.22–0.91, p=0.03**). The combined **CYP2B6*1/*6 + *6/*6** group also showed reduced risk (**OR 0.42, 95% CI 0.21–0.84, p=0.01**). However, **CYP2B6*6/*6 alone was not significantly associated** with NVP-related liver injury (**OR 0.45, 95% CI 0.15–1.32, p=0.14**). Overall, the results support **drug-specific effects of CYP2B6 genotype on liver toxicity risk**, with **CYP2B6*6 increasing EFV-related DILI risk but showing an apparent protective association for NVP in pooled analyses**.
