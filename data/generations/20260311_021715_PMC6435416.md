## Summary

## Background
This retrospective cohort study examined whether **CYP2D6 genotype-predicted metabolizer status** affects the risk of **adverse events (AEs)** during **risperidone** treatment in children and adolescents. Using DNA from a linked biobank and electronic health records, the authors sequenced **CYP2D6**, including assessment of deletions and duplications, and assigned patients to **poor, intermediate, normal, or ultrarapid metabolizer** groups. The analysis included **257 patients** aged 18 years or younger who had taken risperidone for at least 4 weeks.

## Key Findings
The main pharmacogenomic finding was that **reduced CYP2D6 function was associated with more risperidone adverse events**. Because some phenotype groups were small, the authors combined patients into **poor/intermediate metabolizers** (n=33) versus **normal/ultrarapid metabolizers** (n=224). Adverse events occurred in **46%** of poor/intermediate metabolizers (**15/33**) compared with **27%** of normal/ultrarapid metabolizers (**61/224**), which was statistically significant (**P=0.04**).

In multivariable logistic regression adjusting for **age, sex, race, and initial risperidone dose**, **poor/intermediate metabolizers had a 2.4-fold higher odds of adverse events** than normal/ultrarapid metabolizers (**adjusted OR 2.4, 95% CI 1.1–5.1, P=0.03**). The most common AEs overall were **weight change, sedation, and extrapyramidal symptoms**. The study did **not report associations for individual CYP2D6 variants or star alleles separately**; instead, it focused on the **overall CYP2D6 metabolizer phenotype**. Among normal/ultrarapid metabolizers, use of a **strong CYP2D6 inhibitor** was **not associated** with increased AEs (**30% vs 27%, P=0.8**; adjusted OR **1.2**, 95% CI **0.4–3.3**, **P=0.7**).
